Notes

Understanding Widening

Comparing the Galois Connection and Widening/Narrowing Approaches to Abstract Interpretation (Cousot and Cousot; 2005) gives a great description of widening and how it relates to the Galois connection approach. In particular, it shows how the two approaches (might) coincide when the domain is already finite, i.e., has no infinitely ascending chains.

The paper also addresses the question: if the widened domain needs to by finite, why can't you just start with a finite domain in the first place? The paper argues that no single finite domain is sufficient in power to a widening operator parameterized over an arbitrary program. There is intuition given about how a widening operator dynamically selects some finite domain, using information from the partially constructed results of abstract interpretation.

The paper also shows how the iterations strategies for widening resembles that of the standard approach.

The paper also argues that widening operators should be designed with a Galois connection in mind, and that it should be respectful of it. The paper gives a great example of how the standard widening for intervals example does not respect the Galois connection for the finite domain of signs.

Mechanizing fixpoints

Building Certified Static Analysers by Modular Construction of Well-founded Lattices (Pichardie; 2008) gives a great description of mechanizing both Kleene fixpoint iteration and fixpoint iteration with widening. The approach uses the idea of a well-founded relation, which is a useful technique in general for performing non-structural induction in a proof assistant. The paper also describes how to design a suitable well-founded relation from a widening operator.

A Minimalistic Look at Widening Operators (Monniaux; 2009) describes the more general fixpoint iteration technique with widening that is used in Astrée.
